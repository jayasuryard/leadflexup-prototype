# SKILL: Hero Sections That Convert

## Overview
Hero sections are the **first and most critical** part of a landing page. A great hero should 90% convince a visitor to buy — the rest of the page merely clears doubts. This skill defines how to design, write, and structure hero sections for maximum conversion.

---

## Core Principle: Clarity Over Artistry

The hero section's job is NOT to:
- Spark curiosity or be mysterious
- Look beautiful at the cost of meaning
- Use vague, abstract, or "smart-sounding" language

The hero section's job IS to:
- Immediately show the user what problem you solve
- Make the benefit crystal clear in seconds
- Give them a single, obvious next action

> If the visitor doesn't understand the value in the hero, they leave. Sale: lost.

---

## Red Flags to Avoid

### ❌ The "Word Salad" Headline
Vague, AI-generated buzzwords that sound impressive but say nothing:
- *"Seamless", "transformative", "innovative", "leverage", "synergy", "unlock", "empower"*

**Rule:** If your headline contains two or more of these buzzwords, rewrite it entirely.

### ❌ Visuals-First Design
Starting with a beautiful AI-generated image and then fitting words around it results in:
- Messaging shaped by aesthetics, not user needs
- Confusing, decorative copy that doesn't convert
- Glassmorphic menus floating over meaningless landscapes

### ❌ Abstractions Instead of Humans
Using abstract shapes, landscapes, or generic stock photos instead of real human imagery.

---

## The Right Process: Typeframe First

### What is a Typeframe?
A **typeframe** is a low-fidelity wireframe with high-fidelity text. All copy is written, structured, and sized correctly — before any colors or visuals are added.

Coined by Michał Malewicz (Squareblack) around 2016 as a middle path between:
- Full high-fidelity mockups (risky if messaging is wrong)
- Plain wireframes (confusing to non-designers / marketing teams)

### Typeframe Process
1. **Identify WHY** the target customer should care
2. **Write 3–5 versions** of the headline copy
3. **Test locally** (not on the internet) — get real reactions
4. **Nail hierarchy** — use proper font sizes, spacing, and structure
5. Only after copy is approved → move to visual design

> All text decisions happen **before** any colorful pixels go on screen.

---

## Hero Section Structure (5 Elements)

Use **Hierarchy Strips** to evaluate each layer:

| # | Element | Purpose | Tips |
|---|---------|---------|------|
| 1 | **Kicker** | Establish credibility immediately | Use social proof (e.g., "Trusted by 10,000+ businesses") |
| 2 | **Main Headline** | State the core benefit/problem solved | Short, plain language, immediately understood |
| 3 | **Description** | Show HOW you solve it, clear 1–2 doubts | Brief — 1–2 sentences max |
| 4 | **CTA Button(s)** | Drive the conversion action | One primary CTA; add a secondary only for high-ticket/high-friction products |
| 5 | **Key Visual** | Emotionally confirm the message | Human image preferred; shows the problem being solved |

## Visual Design Principles

### Use Humans, Not Abstractions
- 99% of businesses convert better with a human in the hero image
- People naturally align with other people — it's easier to imagine yourself in their shoes
- Emotional connection is essential

**Avoid:**
- Clearly stock photos (kills brand trust)
- Random people who don't relate to the message
- Abstract shapes or AI landscapes

**Best practice:** Use your own photos, heavily-tweaked AI-generated images, or heavily-modified stock photos.

### Color Matching
- Recolor parts of the image (e.g., clothing) to a subtle hue of your CTA color
- This reinforces visual connection between the human and the action
- Only highlight one piece of clothing — don't make it a monochrome uniform
- Keep the recoloring subtle

### The Gaze Principle
- A person looking directly at the camera draws attention to their eyes
- A person looking toward the CTA or headline **shifts attention** to where you want it
- Use gaze direction intentionally to guide the user's eye

### Guiding Lines
- Text line breaks and the diagonal created by right-aligned text form a **natural reading path**
- This path should flow from the person's eyes → headline → description → CTA button
- Broken or misaligned layouts slow this flow and reduce conversions

---

## Copy Rules

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Speak directly to the user's problem | Use vague aspirational language |
| Use plain, industry-appropriate words | Use buzzwords that sound sophisticated |
| Be brief and specific | Over-explain or try to be clever |
| Make the benefit obvious in <5 seconds | Make users scroll to understand the offer |

---

## CTA Strategy

- **Default:** One CTA button leading directly to the sales process
- **High-ticket / high-friction products:** Add a secondary CTA (e.g., "See case studies" or "See how it works") to reduce friction before commitment
- The CTA should be visually reinforced by color matching in the hero image

---

## Summary Checklist

Before finalising any hero section, verify:

- [ ] Headline is free of vague buzzwords
- [ ] Value proposition is clear in under 5 seconds
- [ ] Copy was written before visual design began (typeframe process)
- [ ] Visual confirms and amplifies the copy — not the other way around
- [ ] Human figure used (where applicable) with correct gaze direction
- [ ] Image color-matched subtly to CTA color
- [ ] Kicker uses social proof or credibility signal
- [ ] Single primary CTA (secondary only if high-friction industry)
- [ ] Reading path flows naturally: eyes → headline → description → CTA
